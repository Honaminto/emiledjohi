Climate Extremes and Economic Stressors as Predictors of Conflict in Central America: Evidence from Monthly Data (2012–2024)

Emile Honaminto Djohi
University of Pennsylvania

Working Paper, Draft — December 2025

Abstract: This paper examines how climate extremes influence protest and violence in Central America through economic stress. Using newly assembled monthly data for six countries between 2012 and 2024, the study combines conflict event data (MLP and MLEED), climate indicators (temperature, precipitation, drought), and inflation-based measures of economic pressure. An ElasticNet framework identifies relevant lag structures, and a mediation design decomposes direct and indirect effects. The results show that climate shocks consistently raise inflation, while their direct effects on protest and violence largely disappear once economic channels are accounted for. The findings highlight the central role of economic stress in shaping short-term conflict dynamics in the region.

Keywords: Climate extremes, conflict, economic stress, inflation, Central America, monthly data
